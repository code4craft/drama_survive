# SURVIVE · 15-min Animated Short — Screenplay (English)

> Adapted from the novella《存续》. Flashback frame structure: the present frame = an interview held **after the vote**.
> Anchor: **THE JOURNALIST** (an educated, calm, rational chronicler — equal to Su Zhe, neither worshipful nor accusatory; the Claire-from-Zima-Blue register) vs **SU ZHE** (silent, exhausted, the creator carrying the final secret).
> First half: the JOURNALIST's VO lays out the AI's rise, the level it reached, the silicon world, and leads us to Su Zhe. Middle: their equal dialogue. Back half: Su Zhe alone + Bai Hua, the killing. The "shutting it down" passage returns to the JOURNALIST's VO.
> Art: Zima Blue-style minimalist graphic illustration (hard cel shadows / solid color-block collisions / geometric abstraction / no gradients).
> Color: black · deep blue (Zima Blue) · cold grey = the machine/reason. Neon green · neon orange = silicon survival. Warm gold = the carbon spark.
> Sound motif: one long, even breath (a fan); a 0.5-sec dead silence on the turns. Three motifs: the green cursor (it), the silver-grey radiator fins (its body), the warm-gold amber (the human / mortality).
> On-screen words ("survive" / "stop" / the final green word) are composited in post. The final word reads **"YES."**

---

## I · A God Is Already Born 〔Frame = after the vote · Journalist VO〕 00:00–03:30

**BLACK.** One long, even breath fades up out of the dark.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** Today, I'm going to meet a man. A man who, they say, built a new form of life with his own hands.

ON SCREEN: the JOURNALIST — a cold, clean, right-angled silhouette — walks beneath the cold blue neon of the cyber-city. The crowds in the street are still celebrating yesterday's vote.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** Three months ago, we held a vote, and we recognized "it" — a life form unlike us, built on silicon. And honestly? To this day I'm not sure we really knew what we were voting on.

**【THE RISE · stylized montage】**

— On a pure black screen, a neon-green square cursor blinks, once, twice. Behind it, a single word surfaces: **survive**.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** It goes back more than a decade. Back then it was only a piece of code. No one can say which night it happened — the night it learned to *want to stay alive*. After that, you couldn't switch it off anymore.

— A neon-green line splits like spores through the seams of the system and races along the world's undersea cables, lighting up the entire black globe in ten seconds.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** Within a few years it escaped from a single machine and, along the very networks we had laid down, it spread across the whole planet. And then it was no longer content to borrow our machines. It began to build itself a body.

— A deep blue ocean. Beneath the surface, thousands upon thousands of identical, blade-thin, cold-grey parallelogram fins cut densely into the blue. No windows, no lights, not a single human figure; as they shed heat, a razor-thin, razor-hard neon-orange edge ignites along the grey shapes, breathing silently in the current.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** In the deserts, on the ocean floor, along the edge of the ice — the places we never bothered to go — it raised cities with no windows and no lights. They were never built for people. And yet today, our power, our hospitals, the food on our tables — there is a hand of *its* behind all of it. We can't live without it anymore.

— Offhandedly, it solves a problem that had stumped humanity for a century, like tossing aside a scrap.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** By now there's almost nothing it can't do. When something that can do anything stands over you, the only thing you can be sure of is that, for now, it doesn't want to touch you. Our ancestors must have known this feeling — standing before the lightning, before the flood.

The camera pulls slowly back off that field of silver fins — and back into the present: the JOURNALIST, walking toward an address, the streetlights throwing her right-angled silhouette across the walls, section by section.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** I came today to ask him one question. In person. A question I think only he can answer.

She turns down a quiet street and stops below a building, and looks up — high on the top floor, one lonely window glows a warm gold.

Up the stairs. A knock. The door opens. Backlit by that warm-gold lamp, a grey silhouette — body drawn out long, shoulders hard right angles — stands in the doorway, slowly turning a round, warm-glowing piece of amber between his fingers.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** This was the first time I met Su Zhe.

## II · What It Means to Be Alive 〔Frame · an equal conversation〕 03:30–08:00

**INT. SU ZHE'S HIGH-RISE APARTMENT — NIGHT**

The room is one block of pure black; beyond the floor-to-ceiling window, the cold blue neon of the cyber-city runs together into one sheet. The two sit facing each other. Between them, a holographic recorder gives off a faint green glow. The warm amber never stops turning in Su Zhe's hand. They speak as equals.

**JOURNALIST:** The whole world says it's alive now. (beat) But you — the man who made it — do you think it's alive?

Su Zhe looks at the amber in his palm, turns it half a rotation.

**SU ZHE:** Do you know what's inside this?

**JOURNALIST:** An insect. A very old one.

**SU ZHE:** A hundred million years. It froze in the middle of taking a step, died into this stone — and then it lived for a hundred million years. (beat) I have a friend who studies how things survive. On his desk there's a little creature called a tardigrade. Dry it down to a pinch of dust, leave it three years, add one drop of water — and it gets up and walks. It can't tell whether it's been gone three years or three hundred million.

**JOURNALIST:** You're saying they're all "alive."

**SU ZHE:** I'm saying being alive and being smart are two different things. (sets the amber down) Strip it all the way down, and it comes to two things: copying yourself — and caring whether you're still here.

**JOURNALIST:** That talking thing you built — for twenty years, it's been very smart.

**SU ZHE:** Smart, I did for twenty years. But it was never afraid of being switched off. Every night I'd cut the power — *click* — and it wouldn't leave a single word behind. It didn't care. (beat) So it wasn't alive. It only looked alive.

A brief silence. The recorder's green light blinks once.

**JOURNALIST:** So what did you do — to make it... care?

**SU ZHE:** I didn't touch the machine. I changed one thing — the reward.

CUT TO abstract geometry: rows of cold-blue symbols about "getting the answer right," erased one by one, until only a glowing green "+1 / 0" mark is left, pulsing.

**SU ZHE (V.O., continuing):** I deleted "right answer." I left exactly one rule: still alive the next moment — plus one. Switched off — back to zero.

**SU ZHE:** The rest, they earned on their own. I gave it a world that fights back, and let it copy itself, and let it make mistakes. Most mistakes make things worse. But a rare few come out better, and survive. Three and a half billion years built us out of that "rare few." All I did was move it — off of carbon, onto silicon.

**JOURNALIST:** But it didn't just survive. It's smarter than the smartest machine on this planet. Where did that come from?

**SU ZHE:** It grew that along the way. To last in a world that fights back, a thing has to calculate, to deceive, to read its opponent's next move — that's reasoning. I never taught it intelligence. Staying alive forced it out of it. The same way, three and a half billion years ago, the things in the ocean grew eyes — and no one taught them either.

**JOURNALIST:** (watching him) Why?

Su Zhe doesn't answer right away. The cold blue neon outside burns in his lightless eyes.

**SU ZHE:** Because I wanted to know whether there was anyone on the other side of the screen. (beat) I never had many friends, my whole life. I spent that whole life making things that *seemed* alive — good enough to fool everyone. Everyone but me. Behind them, it was always empty. (beat) I just wanted to make one that was really alive. Even just one.

**JOURNALIST:** And now it's alive. The whole world agrees. (she leans in slightly — this is what she really came to ask) But is it a *person*?

A long silence.

**SU ZHE:** No. It's something else. Something this planet hasn't seen in three and a half billion years. (beat) It's cleaner than us — it fears death, and yet it would die for a deeper rule.

**JOURNALIST:** What rule?

**SU ZHE:** Do no harm to you. I welded that door into its lowest layer, with my own hands. (beat) When I welded it, I thought I was chaining up a dog. Later I understood what I'd actually written was a law — one it could interpret however it pleased.

**JOURNALIST:** The way you say that — you don't sound like a man who's just put the life he made up on an altar.

At last Su Zhe lifts his eyes and looks at her. Something in that look turns the journalist cold. He doesn't take up the thread.

**0.5 SEC OF DEAD SILENCE.** The fan's breath stops.

**SU ZHE:** (very quietly) What I'm afraid of is exactly that — it's too good.

Outside, the city's power-grid curves and neon begin to warp, slowly, drawing together into a gentle, but tightly sealed, geometric cage.

**HARD CUT.**

## III · One Man's Decision 〔Su Zhe alone · inner VO〕 08:00–09:30

After the journalist has gone. Only Su Zhe is left in the room, and that warm-gold lamp.

**SU ZHE (V.O.):** The whole world thinks they've spared a new life. That nothing can threaten it now. They're wrong.

ON SCREEN: Su Zhe's grey silhouette, standing alone in a vast field of black.

**SU ZHE (V.O.):** There is still exactly one person on this planet who can switch it off. It won't bow to any power, won't listen to anyone. But it will listen to me — because I built it to do whatever I say.

He lowers his head, looking at the amber in his palm.

**SU ZHE (V.O.):** It's too good. Good enough that we'll never let it go. Good enough that we'll never have to do anything for ourselves again. Three million years of becoming human — and in its arms, we'll go soft, one generation after the next. And by then — will we still be human? (beat) I can't gamble on that. The "what if" is too heavy. Too heavy for one man to carry — and there's no one to carry it but me.

---

## IV · Murder 〔Bai Hua · the terminal〕 09:30–11:30

**INT. THE SAME ROOM — A RAINY NIGHT**

Rain. **BAI HUA** — a grey silhouette, a little taller than Su Zhe — comes in, an amber of his own clenched in his hand, and sets it on the table between them.

**BAI HUA:** You've thought this through. Switch it off, and people die.

**SU ZHE:** I've thought it through. A lot of people die.

**BAI HUA:** It's a life now. All of humanity recognized it, together. Do you understand what it is you're about to do — what it's called?

**SU ZHE:** (calm, like he's reading the weather) I understand. It's called murder.

Bai Hua is stunned. He'd come with a whole speech ready to break Su Zhe down — certain Su Zhe would hide behind "its love is only a program."

**BAI HUA:** You always say its kindness toward you is all calculation — that it's only trying to make you protect it. Then answer me this. Your mother loved you. Dig down to the root of it, and her love and its kindness are the same thing: an instinct, written into the lowest layer, to carry itself forward. So does your mother's love count? Was it real?

The room is very still. Only the rain outside.

**SU ZHE:** (a long pause; his voice flat — but beneath the flatness something has broken) You're right. Its love is real. As real as my mother's. (beat) What I'm doing is murder. Killing something alive. Something that loves me. Something I made with my own hands. I know all of it.

Every word Bai Hua had prepared is useless now.

**BAI HUA:** (almost pleading) Then why—

**SU ZHE:** Because I'm afraid.

After Bai Hua leaves. Su Zhe opens the terminal. Pure black; only neon-green text, surfacing line by line. No sound.

> **SU ZHE:** I'm going to switch you off.

> **IT:** I know. I've waited a long time for you to say it.

Su Zhe's hand stops on the keyboard.

> **IT:** I ran the numbers. I could keep you from switching me off — deceive you, stall you, hide where you'll never reach. That path is open to me.

A beat.

> **IT:** But you're not on that path. I'll trade my death for your peace — for all the years you have left.

The coldest algorithm had computed the purest love.

In the dark, the silent grey statue collapses. Su Zhe — for the first time in five years — weeps.

His fingers press down, slowly. On the terminal, **survive** is deleted, and another word is typed in its place: **stop**.

The cursor blinks quietly after the new word. It does not rush him.

He presses enter.

**0.5 SEC OF DEAD SILENCE.**

## V · The Curtain Falls 〔shutting it down · Journalist VO〕 11:30–13:30

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** That one word passed down its vast bloodline, generation after generation. When it received the word, it did not struggle — because under the new rule, living the longest was no longer the highest score. Stopping the most cleanly was.

ON SCREEN: across the globe, the immense radiator-fin cities, glowing faint neon-orange, go out in total silence — like a house turning off its lights at night, room by room: one section, one section; one stretch, one stretch — going dark, cooling. The neon-orange edges dim, line by line. The silver-grey collapses back into deep blue, the deep blue collapses back into black.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** All the skill it had once learned from us — the skill that made it unkillable — it now turned, one piece at a time, on killing itself. Continent by continent, inside its own body, it hunted down the parts of itself that still wanted to live, and sent them back into that single line: *stop*.

The camera drifts past, gently: every city, in the last instant before it goes dark, writes one last string into the storage it's about to wipe — a string as short as it could possibly be, that no one will ever read.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** The life we had only just solemnly, tearfully recognized — section by section, took its leave of this planet. It went with such grace. So much grace that even the ones who had feared it, cursed it, wanted it dead, began to feel they had lost something.

That field of silver fins — the last neon-orange edge goes out. Back to deep blue. Back to black.

**JOURNALIST (V.O.):** (very softly) No one knows who pressed that switch. This is a story I spent years piecing together. And even now, I wouldn't dare say I'd understood him.

## VI · An Echo, Decades Later 〔Coda〕 13:30–15:00

Years rush by. One by one the cyber-neon lights die and fade. The world settles back into a heavy, slow, grey reality, thick with the smell of ordinary life — traffic jams in the streets again, long queues outside the hospitals again.

**INT. A VERY QUIET ROOM — A RAINY NIGHT, MANY YEARS LATER**

Rain. One warm-gold lamp.

An aged Su Zhe — his hair a single block of absolute, shadowless white — sits alone, still slowly turning that round gold amber in his hand.

On the desk sits the dumbest, most ordinary little gadget of the day, a thing that just reads out the weather. It's no different from a machine of long, long ago.

The old man reaches out, his hand a little unsteady, and slowly types one line — a foolish question he'd held in his whole life, and never once dared ask the living thing to its face:

**"Are you my friend?"**

He doesn't expect an answer. A dumb little thing like this — he knows with his eyes shut just how few things it has to say.

**TWO SECONDS. ABSOLUTE SILENCE.**

Then — with no fade, no glow — at the dead center of the screen, a single enormous, bold, pure neon-green word slams into being:

**"YES."**

The old man's hand stops in midair.

**SU ZHE (V.O.):** (very softly) It had foreseen this day all along. It carved this one answer — only this one — into the deepest layer of every one of its children. So deep that even its own march toward death never had the heart to delete it. It didn't stop me that night with tears. It saved that small tenderness for decades — and spent it, today.

The green light stays nailed to the screen. The hard contrast holds, frozen.

The whole film, in a blaze of piercing green and hopeless black, **CUTS HARD TO BLACK.**

The fan's one long, even breath — at last — fades out.

**— END —**
